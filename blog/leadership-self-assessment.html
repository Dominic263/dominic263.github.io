Rating Myself Honestly: What Two Leadership Self-Assessments Taught Me About Leading AI Change

AIML-500 · Workshop One · Leadership Discussion — July 2026

This week I completed two self-assessments — one on AI/ML integration leadership skills, and one on change management skills — after reading the Center for Creative Leadership's research on what separates successful change leaders from unsuccessful ones. Putting a number between 1 and 5 next to statements like "I handle budgeting and financial management for AI/ML projects" turns out to be an uncomfortable and useful exercise. Here's what I found.

My Top 3 Strengths

AI/ML Integration Leadership Assessment

  1. Continuous learning. Keeping up with AI/ML advancements was my highest rating. Between my work at the Tufts High Energy Physics Lab and this program, staying current isn't a chore for me — it's the part of the field I enjoy most.
  2. Technical fundamentals and data requirements. My engineering background and daily work with physics simulation data mean I understand what AI/ML systems actually need — clean data, realistic constraints, and honest expectations.
  3. Explaining AI/ML to non-technical audiences. I once built an AR application specifically to teach machine learning concepts to high school students. Translating technical ideas into something people can grab onto is a skill I've deliberately practiced.

Change Management Assessment

  1. Commit. Of CCL's "3 C's," this is my strongest. Endurance sports taught me what commitment looks like when the initial excitement is long gone — and leading change requires exactly that persistence through the unglamorous middle.
  2. Collaborate. From leading a five-intern testing team to our recent task-force-based bot project, I default to bringing people in early rather than presenting them with finished decisions.
  3. Learn. I ask a lot of questions, and I'd rather find out I'm wrong early than defend a bad plan late. The research's finding that "great leaders are great learners" resonated with how I try to operate.

My Top 3 Areas for Growth

AI/ML Integration Leadership Assessment

  1. Budgeting and financial management. My lowest rating. As an early-career engineer, I've simply never owned a project budget — I know how to spend compute efficiently, not how to defend a line item.
  2. Assessing organizational readiness and building training programs. I've prepared myself for AI-related change many times; preparing an entire organization is a different skill I haven't yet exercised.
  3. Regulatory requirements. I understand AI ethics conceptually, but the concrete regulatory landscape — what compliance actually requires, jurisdiction by jurisdiction — is an area where I need real study.

Change Management Assessment

  1. Sway. The research describes mapping stakeholders and defining what "buy-in" looks like for each. I tend to focus on doing convincing work rather than deliberately influencing the people who decide — and those aren't the same thing.
  2. Strategize. I'm comfortable improvising and iterating, but successful change leaders build explicit action plans with priorities, timelines, and metrics — and define success at the start, not along the way.
  3. Support. I hold myself to a high standard and can default to focusing on results. The research is clear that unsuccessful change leaders do exactly that — while successful ones spend their energy removing barriers for others.

My Opinion of the Assessments as Tools

I found them genuinely useful, with real limitations. Their value is specificity: "leadership" is an abstraction, but "I develop comprehensive training programs for AI/ML tools" is a claim you can honestly answer. Breaking a vague identity question into twenty-eight concrete behaviors made my gaps visible in a way general reflection wouldn't have.

The limitations are the ones built into any self-report. A 1–5 scale with no behavioral anchors means my "4" and a classmate's "4" may describe very different abilities. The assessments also can't distinguish low skill from no opportunity — I rated budgeting a 2 not because I've tried and struggled, but because I've never been given a budget. And self-ratings are vulnerable to both modesty and optimism; without outside input, I'm grading my own exam.

Revisions I Would Make

  • Add behavioral anchors. Define what a 1, 3, and 5 actually look like for each item ("5 = I have built and delivered a training program adopted by an organization"), so scores are comparable and honest.
  • Add an evidence line. One sentence per category: "Give a specific example that justifies your rating." A rating you can't evidence is a wish, not an assessment.
  • Add a "no opportunity yet" option. Distinguishing "I've never done this" from "I do this poorly" would make results far more actionable, especially for early-career professionals.
  • Add a 360° component. Even one peer rating alongside the self-rating would expose blind spots — which is where the most important growth areas usually hide.
  • Weight by role. A researcher, a manager, and an executive shouldn't be scored on the same flat scale; letting respondents mark which items are core to their current role would sharpen the "areas for growth" signal.

The overall lesson I'm taking into the rest of this program: my technical foundation and persistence are real assets, but leading AI/ML change is mostly a people discipline — influence, support, and deliberate strategy — and that's exactly where my deliberate practice needs to go.

Referenced: Center for Creative Leadership — How to Be a Successful Change Leader (the 9 competencies and 3 C's of change leadership). Written for the AIML-500 Workshop One Leadership Discussion at Indiana Wesleyan University.
