Professional Bio

Engineer. Physics researcher. AI builder. Endurance athlete. One discipline, four laboratories.

My Journey

I started as a robotics and mechanical engineer, earning my B.S. in Mechanical Engineering with a minor in Computer Science at Tufts University. Building physical systems taught me to respect constraints: materials fail, tolerances matter, and an elegant design that can't survive the real world isn't a design at all. Along the way I led robotics fabrication projects, built AR applications that taught machine learning to high school students, and helped test PyScript for robotics and IoT as an intern with Tufts CEEO and Anaconda. That respect for rigor eventually pulled me toward one of the hardest proving grounds in science — high-energy physics.

What I Do

Today I'm a software engineer at the Tufts University High Energy Physics Lab, working on the DUNE project (Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment) — one of the largest neutrino physics experiments in the world. I write software to run physics simulations in object-oriented C++ and ROOT, and I've fabricated 3D models for the first prototype of a muon-neutrino detector for the Tufts Neutrino Group — engineering design, software, and fabrication in one job.

AI & Machine Learning

AI is where my engineering and research worlds converge, and it's the center of where I'm headed. I'm pursuing graduate study in AI and machine learning at Indiana Wesleyan University, and I don't just study it — I ship it. I've built and deployed machine learning models (linear regression and KNN) inside an augmented reality app that taught ML fundamentals to high school students, co-built a knowledge-grounded conversational AI agent that recommends speakers from verified profiles without hallucinating, and hand-coded an interactive visual history of the field. The datasets experiments like DUNE produce are exactly the kind of massive, messy, high-stakes data modern ML was made for — bringing those two worlds together is the work I want to spend my career on.

Beyond the Lab

Outside the lab, I've spent years chasing a different kind of edge: kayaking in unpredictable open water, long days hiking, high-altitude summits, and the physical exhaustion that forces a choice between quitting and continuing. I treat mountains, water, and endurance challenges as my own personal laboratory for testing what the mind and body can actually withstand. I speak from the conviction that the discipline required to run a rigorous physics experiment and the discipline required to survive a brutal climb are, underneath, the same discipline.

I also build for fun — and finish what I build. My latest side project, Mochi: Word Party, is a multiplayer word-guessing game live on the App Store: guess the secret word in 20 questions, outsmart your friends, and pick up a new word every day.

Aspirations

I want to build my career at the intersection of hardware and software — the place where code meets the physical world — leveraging artificial intelligence to create robust, resilient systems that hold up outside the lab. Whether it's a neutrino detector, a robot arm helping farmers plant seedlings, or an intelligent assistant people actually rely on, I'm drawn to problems where rigor is non-negotiable and the result has to work in the real world. My goal is to bring machine learning to the kinds of massive, messy datasets that experiments like DUNE produce, and to help teams turn ambitious questions into reliable, working systems.
