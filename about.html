Professional Bio

Engineer. Physics researcher. Endurance athlete. One discipline, three laboratories.

My Journey

I started my career as a robotics and mechanical engineer, with a foundation in both mechanical engineering and computer science. Building physical systems taught me to respect constraints: materials fail, tolerances matter, and an elegant design that can't survive the real world isn't a design at all. That respect for rigor eventually pulled me toward one of the hardest proving grounds in science — high-energy physics.

What I Do

Today I work as a researcher on the DUNE project (Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment) — one of the largest neutrino physics experiments in the world. My working life sits inside one of physics' most ambitious open questions: what neutrinos can tell us about the universe's most basic rules. Alongside that, I'm pursuing graduate study in artificial intelligence and machine learning at Indiana Wesleyan University, building the skills to bring modern AI methods to large-scale scientific and engineering problems.

Beyond the Lab

Outside the lab, I've spent years chasing a different kind of edge: high-altitude mountain summits, unpredictable open water, and the physical exhaustion that forces a choice between quitting and continuing. I treat mountains, open water, and endurance challenges as my own personal laboratory for testing what the mind and body can actually withstand. I speak from the conviction that the discipline required to run a rigorous physics experiment and the discipline required to survive a brutal climb are, underneath, the same discipline.

Aspirations

My goal is to work at the intersection of experimental science and artificial intelligence — applying machine learning to the kinds of massive, messy datasets that experiments like DUNE produce, and helping research and technology teams turn ambitious questions into reliable, working systems.
